The BDM Expected Utility Model

A proven methodology for predicting outcomes of strategic interactions — from geopolitics to business deals. Created by Bruce Bueno de Mesquita with a claimed 90%+ accuracy rate across thousands of predictions.

1 What Is Game Theory Prediction?
lightbulb The Core Idea

Imagine you could predict the outcome of a negotiation, political decision, or business deal before it happens — and then figure out exactly what to change to get the result you want.

That's what this model does. It takes a complex situation with multiple people or organizations ("players"), each with their own goals and power, and simulates how they'll influence each other until the situation settles into an outcome.

🌊
Think of it Like Weather
Meteorologists don't guess — they model how air pressure, temperature, and humidity interact. We do the same with people's positions, power, and priorities.
♟️
Strategic Chess
Every player makes moves based on what they think will happen. The model calculates all possible moves simultaneously to find where everything converges.
🧲
Magnetic Pull
Each player is like a magnet pulling the outcome toward their preferred position. Stronger magnets (more power + more motivation) pull harder.

looks_one Step 1: Define the Issue

Every prediction starts with a question on a 0-100 scale. The two ends represent the extreme possible outcomes.

Example: "What will the final car price be?" where 0 = dealer invoice cost and 100 = full sticker price. The status quo (where things are today before negotiation) might be 75.

looks_two Step 2: Identify Every Player

List everyone who can influence the outcome — not just the obvious ones. Include indirect players like competitors, regulators, market forces, and public opinion.

The model needs at least 3 players to work. Real scenarios often have 5-20+.

3 The Five Core Parameters
🎯
Position
"Where do they want things to land?"
0 — One extreme100 — Other extreme

Their ideal outcome on the 0-100 scale. A buyer wanting the lowest price: 15-25. A seller wanting full price: 80-90.

⚡
Capability
"How much power do they have?"
0 — No influence100 — Dominant

Relative power to shape the outcome. A CEO: 75-90. A mid-level manager: 30-45. Even the US in global scenarios rates 70-85, not 100.

🔥
Salience
"How much do they care?"
0 — Don't care100 — Top priority

A powerful player who doesn't care won't engage. Capability × Salience = actual influence. This is the key insight of BDM.

🤸
Flexibility
"Will they compromise?"
0 — Completely rigid100 — Very flexible

How much they'll budge under pressure. A government with a mandate: 10-20. A dealmaker: 60-75. Rigid players hold firm but risk isolation.

🎲
Risk Profile
"How do they handle uncertainty?"

Risk Averse 🛡 — plays it safe, avoids confrontation
Risk Neutral ⚖️ — plays the odds rationally (most common)
Risk Acceptant 🎲 — swings for the fences, challenges aggressively

key The Key Insight

The most important thing BDM discovered: it's not just about who has the most power. A player with moderate power who cares deeply about the issue (high salience) will often outmaneuver a more powerful player who has other priorities. Think of a small country fighting for survival vs. a superpower that's distracted by 50 other issues.

4 Inside the Simulation Engine
calculate Expected Utility (EU) Calculation

For every pair of players, the engine asks: "If Player A challenges Player B, what's the expected payoff?"

// For each player pair (i challenges j):

EU = P(success) × U(win) + P(failure) × U(lose) - U(status_quo)

// Where:
P(success) = support for i's position / total engaged capability
U(win) = distance between positions × salience // bigger gap = bigger prize
U(lose) = cost of failed challenge // adjusted by risk profile

// If EU > 0: Player i challenges Player j (it's worth the risk)
// If EU ≤ 0: Player i stays quiet (not worth it)
loop Round-by-Round Simulation

The engine runs multiple rounds (typically 5-7). Each round:

1️⃣ Calculate all pairwise EUs — every player vs. every other player
2️⃣ Identify challenges — wherever EU > 0, a challenge occurs
3️⃣ Accumulate pressure — each challenged player receives pressure proportional to challengers' power × motivation
4️⃣ Shift positions — players under pressure move toward their challengers, moderated by their flexibility and salience (stubborn players move less)
5️⃣ Check for convergence — if nobody moves more than 0.5 units, the simulation stops

5 How the Final Prediction Is Made
gps_fixed Power-Weighted Median

The primary prediction is the power-weighted median — the position on the scale where 50% of the total (capability × salience) weight lies on each side. This is more robust than a simple average because it isn't skewed by outliers.

verified Confidence Score

The confidence score (0-100%) reflects two things:

Convergence (60% weight) — did players cluster toward consensus? Tighter clustering = higher confidence.

Coalition Strength (40% weight) — what percentage of total power supports the predicted outcome? A dominant winning coalition = higher confidence.

A confidence of 70%+ is strong. Below 40% means the outcome is fragile and could shift with small parameter changes.

balance Stability Assessment

Stable — players converged, strong coalition. The prediction is reliable.
Fragile — weak convergence, outcome could tip either way. Worth running "what-if" scenarios.
Deadlocked — two balanced opposing blocs can't move each other. Indicates a stalemate requiring external intervention.

6 Why Does This Actually Work?
history_edu Track Record

Bruce Bueno de Mesquita has used this model for over 40 years, making predictions for the CIA, the US Department of Defense, Fortune 500 companies, and governments worldwide. A declassified CIA study found his model accurate in over 90% of cases.

🧠
Rational Actor Theory
People act in their self-interest. They challenge when the expected payoff is positive and stay quiet when it isn't. This simple assumption is remarkably predictive.
🔄
Iterative Convergence
Real negotiations aren't one-shot — they involve rounds of back-and-forth. The model captures this dynamic process rather than just static snapshots.
📊
All Players Matter
Experts tend to focus on the 2-3 most visible players. This model considers everyone — including indirect influences that often tip the balance.
7 Outcome Engineering — Changing the Future
build Not Just Prediction — Strategy

The real power isn't just predicting what will happen — it's figuring out what to change to get the outcome you want. This is called outcome engineering.

🎯
Shift a Position
Convince a key player to change their preferred outcome. "Show the sales manager data that a lower price leads to more volume."
💪
Change Capability
Increase your own power or reduce someone else's. "Get pre-approved financing so you can walk away from any deal."
🔥
Raise Salience
Make the issue more important to a player who'd support you. "Emphasize to the competing dealer that you're a serious buyer."
🤝
Build Coalitions
Align with other players to create a winning bloc. "Use online pricing data + competing quotes to form a price coalition."
8 Glossary
BDM
Bruce Bueno de Mesquita — the political scientist who created this model in the 1980s.
Expected Utility (EU)
The calculated payoff of a player challenging another. If EU > 0, the challenge is rational.
Scenario
The overall situation being modeled — e.g., "Buying a new car" or "EU trade policy reform."
Issue
The specific decision or outcome being predicted, defined on a 0-100 scale with labeled endpoints.
Player / Stakeholder
Any individual, group, or entity that can influence the outcome — including indirect players.
Position
Where a player wants the outcome to land on the 0-100 scale. Their ideal result.
Capability
A player's relative power to influence the outcome compared to others.
Salience
How much a player cares about this specific issue. High salience = they'll fight for it.
Flexibility
Willingness to compromise. Flexible players shift under pressure; rigid players hold firm.
Risk Profile
How a player handles uncertainty — risk averse (safe), neutral (rational), or acceptant (aggressive).
Status Quo
Where things stand today if nobody acts. The starting point on the issue scale.
Convergence
When all players' positions stabilize and nobody moves significantly. The simulation stops here.
Deadlock
Two balanced opposing blocs that can't move each other. Indicates a stalemate.
Coalition
A group of players whose positions cluster together, forming a combined power bloc.
Power-Weighted Median
The predicted outcome position where 50% of (capability × salience) weight is on each side.
Outcome Engineering
The practice of using model insights to strategically change parameters and shift the predicted outcome.
