psychology Scenario Thinking Guide

A step-by-step guide to framing any decision, negotiation, or conflict as a BDM prediction scenario.

lightbulb The Mindset Shift

Most people think about decisions as "what should I do?" The BDM model asks a different question: "Given what every stakeholder wants and how much power they have, where will things land?"

This guide will teach you how to think in that framework — decomposing any situation into issues, players, and numbers so the engine can predict and advise.

1 How to Spot a Scenario

A BDM scenario is any situation where multiple people or groups have different preferences about an outcome and some ability to influence what happens. Ask yourself:

🤝
Am I Negotiating?
Any negotiation — salary, contract, purchase price, custody, lease terms — is a scenario. There's a range of possible outcomes and multiple parties pulling in different directions.
🗳️
Is a Group Deciding?
Board votes, committee decisions, family choices, team strategy — whenever a group must converge on one answer from many opinions, that's a scenario.
⚔️
Is There a Conflict?
Legal disputes, political standoffs, competitive strategy, territorial conflicts — any situation with opposing interests and uncertain resolution.
🔮
Am I Trying to Predict?
Will this policy pass? Will this deal close? Where will this market settle? If you're trying to predict the outcome of a multi-party dynamic, model it.
check_circle Good Scenarios
  • Buying a car (clear negotiation, multiple stakeholders)
  • Office relocation (group decision, competing interests)
  • Trade policy reform (many parties, measurable outcome)
  • Salary negotiation (you, manager, HR, budget)
  • Custody arrangement (parents, lawyers, judge)
cancel Poor Scenarios
  • What should I eat for lunch? (no stakeholder conflict)
  • Will it rain tomorrow? (no human players)
  • Is this code bug-free? (not a negotiation)
  • What's 2+2? (no uncertainty or opposing interests)
  • Solo decisions with no external influence
2 How to Define Your Issue

The issue is the specific question you're trying to predict. The key is framing it as a spectrum with two clear endpoints on a 0–100 scale.

help What is the core decision or outcome at stake?
Strip away the noise. What's the one thing that, when resolved, settles the situation? "How much will I pay?" "What policy will be adopted?" "Who gets custody?"
help What are the two extreme possible outcomes?
These become your 0 and 100. Pick endpoints that are realistic extremes — not impossible fantasies, but the furthest reasonable positions. Example: 0 = "dealer invoice cost" and 100 = "full MSRP" for a car purchase.
help Where do things stand today?
This is the status quo — what happens if nobody acts. It's your starting point. If you walk into a car dealership without negotiating, you'd pay close to sticker price — so status quo might be 75-80.

info Multi-Issue Scenarios

Some situations have more than one issue at stake. A car negotiation might have price AND trade-in value AND interest rate. You can model each as a separate issue. Players have positions on each one. Start with one issue and add more if needed.

3 How to Identify the Players

This is where most people go wrong — they only think of the obvious 2-3 players. The BDM model works best when you capture all sources of influence, including indirect ones.

person_search The Player Discovery Framework

Ask yourself each of these questions to find players you might have missed:

help Who has DIRECT power over the outcome?
The primary decision-makers. The buyer and seller. The judge. The CEO. These are obvious — start here.
help Who can INFLUENCE the decision-makers?
Advisors, managers, spouses, lobbyists, media. The sales manager who approves the price. The spouse who has to agree. The board member who shapes the CEO's thinking.
help Who provides ALTERNATIVES or COMPETITION?
Competitors, alternative options, market forces. The other dealership. The competing job offer. The rival bidder. Even if they're not at the table, they exert pressure.
help Who REGULATES or CONSTRAINS the outcome?
Government agencies, legal requirements, policies, unions. The attorney general who must approve a merger. The zoning board. OSHA. The judge's precedent constraints.
help Who sets EXPECTATIONS or ANCHORS?
Data sources, benchmarks, public opinion. KBB/TrueCar for car prices. Glassdoor for salaries. Industry standards. Public sentiment. These aren't negotiators, but they anchor what everyone considers reasonable.

4 How to Set the Five Numbers

This is the hardest part — but also where the model shines. You're translating messy human judgments into precise numbers. Here's how to think about each one.

gps_fixed
Position (0–100)
"Where does this player want the outcome to land?"

Think of it as: If this player could dictate the outcome unilaterally, where would they put it on the scale?

How to estimate:

  • What has this player publicly stated or demanded?
  • What would serve their self-interest best?
  • Look at their past behavior in similar situations
  • For institutions/data sources: what does the data say?

Capability (0–100)
"How much power do they have to shape the outcome?"

Think of it as: If there were a tug-of-war between all players, how much rope could this player pull?

Key principle: This is RELATIVE. All players' capabilities are compared against each other. Don't default to 50 for everyone — spread them out.

RangeDescriptionSignals
80–100Dominant — near-veto powerFinal approval authority, can unilaterally block or force outcome

40–59Moderate influenceHas a seat at the table, voice is heard but not decisive
20–39Limited but realIndirect leverage, can cause problems or provide alternatives
1–19Minimal — mostly symbolicPublic opinion, background data, weak stakeholders
warning Common Mistake

Even the United States in a global geopolitical scenario typically rates 70–85, not 100. Nobody has absolute power. If you rate someone at 100, you're saying the outcome is predetermined — why bother modeling?

whatshot
Salience (0–100)
"How much do they care about this specific issue?"

Think of it as: Out of everything on this player's plate right now, how much attention and energy will they devote to THIS issue?

This is the BDM secret weapon. A powerful player with low salience is like a sleeping giant — they could influence the outcome but won't because they have bigger priorities.

Capability × Salience = Actual Influence. This product is what really matters.

help Ask yourself these questions:
• Is this issue their #1 priority, or one of many?
• Do they have a personal stake (money, reputation, career)?
• How much time are they spending on this vs. other things?
• Would they fight hard or give in to focus on something else?
• Is there a deadline creating urgency for them?

Flexibility (0–100)
"How willing are they to compromise under pressure?"

Think of it as: When other players push back, does this player dig in or start moving toward a middle ground?

Flexibility affects how fast positions shift during the simulation. Rigid players hold their ground longer but may end up isolated. Flexible players adapt but may concede too much.

help Factors that increase flexibility:
• No hard constraints (can offer a range, not a fixed number)
• Values the relationship over this specific outcome
• Has a weak BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement)
• Under time pressure to resolve
• Open-minded personality or collaborative culture
help Factors that decrease flexibility:
• Bound by policy, law, or mandate (a regulator enforcing a rule)
• Public commitment to a position (can't back down without losing face)
• Strong BATNA (can easily walk away)
• Ideological conviction (this is a matter of principle)
• Data-based anchor (the number is what it is — like KBB pricing)
casino
Risk Profile
"How do they handle uncertain confrontations?"

Think of it as: When the expected utility of challenging another player is uncertain, does this player lean toward caution or aggression?

🛡️
Risk Averse
Plays it safe. Would rather take a guaranteed smaller win than gamble on a bigger one. Only challenges when the odds are strongly favorable.

Who: Banks, insurance companies, career bureaucrats, anyone with a lot to lose.
⚖️
Risk Neutral
Plays the odds. Weighs potential gains and losses equally. Challenges whenever expected value is positive — the rational default.

Who: Data-driven decision-makers, experienced negotiators, most professionals. When in doubt, use this.
🎲
Risk Acceptant
Swings for the fences. Willing to risk a loss for the chance of a big win. Challenges aggressively even when odds are uncertain.

Who: Startup founders, political mavericks, activists, desperate players with nothing to lose.
5 Putting It All Together
checklist Pre-Simulation Checklist
  • Issue defined? — Clear 0–100 scale with labeled endpoints and a status quo position
  • 3+ players identified? — Including indirect influencers, not just the obvious ones
  • All positions set? — Every player has a number for every issue
  • Capabilities spread out? — Not everyone at 50. Someone should be high, someone low
  • Salience varies? — Not everyone cares equally. Who's most invested?
  • Flexibility makes sense? — Anchors are rigid, dealmakers are flexible
  • Risk profiles considered? — Most are neutral, but who's cautious and who's aggressive?
tips_and_updates Pro Tips
  • Don't overthink exact numbers. Being in the right range (±10) matters more than hitting the exact number. The model is robust to small variations.
  • Use the AI Research button. For real-world scenarios, let the AI research players and suggest parameters.
  • Run multiple simulations. Use "What-If" branches to test different assumptions and compare outcomes.
  • Look at the Stakeholder Map. If all your bubbles are in the same area, you might be missing a dissenting voice.
  • Trust the model's surprises. When the prediction differs from your gut feeling, investigate why — the model may be seeing something you're not.
  • Iterate. Your first scenario won't be perfect. Run it, read the analysis, adjust parameters, and run again.
